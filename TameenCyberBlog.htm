Is Zero-Trust Architecture the future of Cybersecurity?

How viable is it?

keywords: Zero-trust, networks, data breaches

As the world becomes more technologically advanced, malicious actors are finding new ways to compromise sensitive data of organizations and costing them money. Recently, there has been a 358% increase of malware attacks from 2019 to 2020 (Griffiths, 2023), and on average, data breaches cost businesses $4.35 million (Griffiths, 2023). This highlights the importance of being able to disrupt such attacks and potentially save millions of dollars for businesses annually. A framework that has been garnering more attention is Zero-Trust Architecture (ZTA). The ZTA framework fundamentally requires real-time visibility and active monitoring of users accessing, or attempting to access the network.

In theory, implementing ZTA will significantly reduce data breaches, since at least 80% of all cyber attacks involve credentials use (Raina, 2023). With ZTA, users attempting to connect to a network would be required to provide and match authorized credentials, including geo location, firmware versions, and even more in-depth identity attributes such as behavioral patterns like typing speed (Ladstaetter, 2023). Therefore, this would minimize the likelihood of cyberattacks through credential misuse, since any mismatch would cease authorization.

However, ZTA has some difficulties for businesses to implement. Probably the most significant struggle is being able to accurately identify organizational workflow so that operations are not disrupted while implementing the framework (Qureshi, 2020).This would require constant oversight so that users are not disrupted by authentication issues. Implementing the ZTA would benefit from microsegmentation of the network (for example, based on departments) to preserve regular workflow. Unfortunately, this would probably lead to the implementation taking much longer, and therefore being more costly.

Therefore, it is vital that cyberprofessionals looking to pitch ZTA to company executives, and perhaps request the hiring of a Chief Zero Trust Officer (CZTO), include a cost-benefit analysis of the money that is being saved from avoiding data breaches, in order to make the argument for a ZTA framework more convincing if it is necessary (Qureshi, 2020). Finally, there should be large considerations of how a ZTA framework would impact employees directly who have to interact with the network, as there would likely be frustrations and inconveniences with verifying a user account, particularly when trying to access the network on different devices - this makes the experience much less flexible for workers. Some businesses who operate on or utilize digital supply chains, such as third party vendors, may not be able to implement ZTA architecture, since verifying that many users would be inconvenient (Yaari, 2023).

Securing a Home Network

Home networks, encryption, security

Internet and network access has become increasingly essential to everyday people, where in 2018-2019, 94% of Canadians had home internet access, with the majority of those who didn't citing cost and unavailability and reasons why they didnt(Statistics Canada 2019). Despite some major data breaches, including the ChatGPT breach that leaked personal data of users, as well as the Western Digital Data Breach on April 3 that gave hackers access to numerous cloud systems, many owners of home networks feel that it is not as necessary to secure their home cybersecurity. However, a secure network would at the minimum prevent unwanted or freeloading users on the network, and in more serious contexts, may proect an individual's sensitive data related to credit card numbers or privileged credentials (Anders, 2023).

However, users can improve and secure their home network with a number of simple steps. Firstly, changing the name of the default home network provides increased protection, since by default, most routers are named after the manufacturer and may even include a model number (Kapersky). Similarly, these routers also come with preset generic passwords that can be discovered online, and so changing the password to something else would be beneficial (Anders, 2023). It is recommended that the password be at least 12 characters long, incorporating special symbols and numbers for increased strength (Kapersky, 2023).

Finally, enabling encryption on the wireless router, eiher to Wifi Protected Access 2 (WPA 2) or WPA 3 (the number signifying different versions. Enabling WPA 2 for example ensures that only the user's device and the router can read what is being transmitted (Kaspersky, 2023). While there are additional security measures for reinforcing a home network, these are the ones I find to be the most important and necessary for basic and admissible home network security.
